[Interview] How to reach 154% of its goal on KickStarter?

Following the recent post listing the “10 Most  successful Canadian Kickstarter Campaigns” we are proud to have the exclusive interview of Jonathan Lavigne, co-founder of Tribute Games Studio.

Tribute Games has recently conducted the 6th most successful Canadian campaign on Kickstarter for their game “Mercenary King”. Indeed they raised more than $110,000 during a 30-days campaign in August-September 2012, achieving 154% of their initial goal!

The following in-depth interview is full of precious details for anyone willing to launch a crowdfunding campaign.

 

Seeding Factory : Hello Jonathan, could you quickly present Tribute Games Studio ?

Jonathan Lavigne: Tribute Games was founded in May 2011 by Jean-François Major, Justin Cyr and myself. We previously worked together at Gameloft and Ubisoft on various 2D games.

However, our “old-school” tastes and skill sets didn’t really match the kind of projects usually developed by these types of traditional game companies. So, about a year after leaving Ubisoft, we decided to join forces to make our own games. Since then, Justin has left us to work on other projects, but some new awesome folks are now joining the team (like Stéphane Boutin and Paul Robertson).

 

S.F. : Why did you decided to go KickStarter  and how the money raised is going to be spend?

J.L.: A game production is really risky because you don’t get any revenue during the whole production cycle. So, the first few steps when starting up a game company are high and crucial. In our case, we had already developed a game called Wizorb and it had good sales, but since we’re selling it for $3, it didn’t generate enough revenue to finance a larger production.

Kickstarter is perfect for game development because it allows game developers to get a revenue during the production. It also forces the developers to clarify their game concept early on and helps them promoting it.

A good chunk of the fund raised will be used to hire an extra programmer. The rest will be used to support various aspects of the production like buying a sound bank, pay for small contracts for additional art and audio assets, help pay our bills, help pay salaries, allow us to show the game at MIGS conference, etc.

 

> Before the release of Mercenary King in the next few months you are invited to download the already available 2D-like game WIZORB.

 

S.F. : Did you seek for other sources of funding before KickStarter?

We were actually evaluating  the possibility of having a publisher to finance a part of the production, but since Mercenary Kings is such in an early stage (it’s currently in a prototype stage), publishers are kind of hesitant to invest in it. They probably prefer cases where the game is already finished and they only need to bring it to the marketplace and take a share.

 

S.F. : How did you prepare and manage the campaign ?

J.L.: It took a whole month to prepare. I looked at various successful campaigns to see what worked and I started from there. We chose the $75,000 goal because it was the strict minimum amount required for our budget to work. And we had to take into account the taxes, Amazon fees, Kickstarter fees and the cost of rewards, which all combined together cut about 40%-50% of what you’re asking for.

About rewards, we tried to go with digital content as much as possible in order to avoid spending all the fund raised into manufacturing and shipping swag. Some people were really demanding and expected much more on the reward side, but in the end, I’m glad that we stayed firm on our position, because it potentially saved us a lot of time and money.

When doing a Kickstarter campaign it essential that you constantly talk about it. We emailed journalists, we posted on forums, Facebook and Twitter. And maintaining it was a full time job because we had to prepare at least 2 updates per week and reply to tons of emails, messages and comments
